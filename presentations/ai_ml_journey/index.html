Debrief — one defensible read each

Open after discussion. These are teaching labels, not a grading key.

Traffic lights (timing + loops)

Mostly rules. Phases and safety interlocks are engineered; sensors trigger transitions but don’t replace the rule graph. Adaptive systems add policy logic — still often rule-forward.

Spam filter (labeled mail)

Mostly learned. The boundary is fit from examples (and updated); hand-written rules may wrap it, but the “smarts” are statistical.

Fraud “unusual spend”

Mix. Hard thresholds and blocklists are rules; risk scoring from transaction history is learned. Products combine both.

Face grouping in photos

Mostly learned. Embeddings / similarity are fit from data; UX rules decide what to show, but grouping quality is ML-driven.

Thermostat schedule

Mostly rules. If you set times and setpoints explicitly, behavior is largely deterministic. “Smart” comfort learning shifts toward mix.

Maps ETA

Mix. Routing constraints + map graph are structured rules; live speeds and demand are learned from telemetry and history.

Paragraph-drafting assistant

Mostly learned. Core text is from a model fit on large corpora; product guardrails add rules on top — still learned-first in practice.

Chapter 2

Two families you’ll hear about

  • Rule-heavy: explicit if-this-then-that logic (great when requirements are stable).
  • Data-heavy (ML): behavior is shaped by examples, evaluation, and iteration.
  • Real products often mix both — the question is where the uncertainty lives.
Chapter 3

The machine learning loop

Chapter 3

Train vs test (intuition)

  • Training: adjust internal knobs to fit examples you’re allowed to learn from.
  • Testing / held-out: check behavior on fresh examples you didn’t train on.
  • If you only ever grade on homework you memorized, you don’t know if you generalize.
Chapter 3

Overfitting (one slide)

A model can become too faithful to training quirks — great on yesterday’s data, brittle tomorrow. Regularization, more diverse data, and honest evaluation help.

Generative AI

  • Discriminative models lean toward labels, scores, or decisions.
  • Generative models synthesize new samples in media space (text, images, audio, …).
  • Responsible use still needs policy, testing, and human review — especially in high-stakes settings.

Chapter 6

Large language models

  • Text is chunked into tokens (words, subwords, symbols).
  • Training objective (simplified): predict a plausible next token given context.
  • At inference: sample/decode repeatedly → paragraphs, code, plans.
